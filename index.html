The 6-point rubric

Where the points actually live

Every FRQ is scored out of 6 on the same three rows. Notice the imbalance — evidence & commentary is worth four times what your thesis is. Spend your time accordingly.

1of 6 pts

Thesis

One defensible claim that answers the prompt. Easy to earn — don't fumble it.

4of 6 pts

Evidence & Commentary

The whole ballgame. Specific evidence plus reasoning that explains why it proves your claim.

1of 6 pts

Sophistication

The bonus point for genuine complexity — not for big vocabulary.

  • Must be defensible — a reasonable person could disagree
  • Must answer the prompt, not dodge it

Evidence & Commentary

4 points ↻ What earns it

To climb the rows

  • Evidence must be specific, not vague gestures
  • Commentary explains why it proves the claim
  • Summary alone does not score highly
  • A clear line of reasoning ties it together
ROW C

Sophistication

1 point ↻ What earns it

To earn the point

  • Shows complexity — tension, nuance, or stakes
  • Handles a counterargument fairly
  • Connects ideas to a broader situation
  • Does not come from fancy words alone
✕ Weak thesis

"There are many reasons social media is both good and bad for teenagers."

Restates the prompt, picks no side, and promises a list. A grader can't tell what you'll argue.

✓ Strong thesis

"Because it rewards outrage over accuracy, social media harms teens less by wasting time than by reshaping what they believe is true."

Defensible, specific, and shows a line of reasoning. The whole essay now has a job to do.

FRQ 2 · Rhetorical Analysis

Stop listing devices. Start explaining choices.

Rhetorical analysis is not a scavenger hunt for metaphors. It's explaining how a writer's choices help them achieve their purpose with a specific audience.

First, map the rhetorical situation

S

Who is talking, and what's their credibility or stake?

A

The four questions to ask every passage

  • Who is speaking, and why should we listen to them?
  • Who are they speaking to?
  • What do they want the audience to think, feel, or do?
  • What choices do they make to achieve that purpose?

PROMPT · Analyze the rhetorical choices the writer makes to argue that public libraries deserve continued funding.

Facing a city council ready to treat libraries as a budget line rather than a public good, the writer argues that libraries earn their funding not as relics but as engines of opportunity. By moving from a single child's story to the scale of an entire community, and by shifting her tone from warm to unflinching, she reframes funding as a moral choice rather than a financial one.

She opens with an anecdote about a girl who learned to read at a branch library that "smelled like rain and old paper." The detail is small and sensory, and that is precisely the point.

By grounding an abstract budget question in one specific child, the writer makes the stakes impossible to dismiss as statistics; the reader who pictures that girl cannot easily vote to close her library. The anecdote does the quiet work of turning policy into a person.

Midway through, her tone hardens. She drops the warm imagery and lists, in flat parallel clauses, what disappears when a library closes: "no printer for the job application, no warm room in January, no one to ask."

The shift from sentiment to blunt inventory mirrors her argument: nostalgia alone won't save the library, so she forces the council to see the concrete services real people lose. The parallel syntax makes each loss land like a separate blow, and the plainness of the language refuses the reader any comfortable distance.

What makes the passage persuasive is not that it chooses emotion over logic, but that it refuses to separate them: the girl and the job application are the same argument, viewed up close and from a distance. In doing so the writer answers the council's likely objection — that feelings are a luxury budgets can't afford — by showing that the feeling is the data.

Thesis · 1 pt

Defensible & specific

Names the purpose and previews the choices — "reframes funding as a moral choice." A grader knows exactly what's coming.

Evidence

Specific textual detail

Quotes the actual language ("smelled like rain") instead of vaguely gesturing at "imagery."

Commentary

Why it works

Explains the effect on the reader and ties it back to purpose — the heart of the 4-point row.

Sophistication · 1 pt

Genuine complexity

Handles tension (emotion vs. logic) and answers an objection — not fancy words, real nuance.

FRQ 1 · Synthesis

Your argument. Their sources.

Synthesis is not a tour of the sources one by one. It's your own argument, with the sources brought in as support exactly where you need them.

Prompt: Should schools adopt a four-day school week? Don't read these sources one at a time — group them by idea.

Now each body paragraph is built around an idea, pulling from whichever sources fit — not "Source A says… Source B says…". That single move is the difference between a 3 and a 6.

The strategy, in order

Five steps before you write

Read the prompt first

Decide what it's actually asking you to take a position on before a single source colors your thinking.

Skim sources for positions

You're hunting for stances, not summaries. Jot a one-word lean next to each.

Group sources by idea

Cluster them like you just did above. Your groups become your body paragraphs.

Write your own thesis

Commit to a position. The sources support you — you don't referee them.

Use sources as support, never as the whole argument

At least three sources, woven in where they earn their place. Cite, then explain in your own voice.

Citing like a pro

Three ways to bring a source in

Introduce

"According to Source A, four-day weeks cut district transportation costs significantly."

Use data

"The five-year data in Source C shows test scores holding steady."

Complicate

"Source F complicates this optimism by showing which students lose a guaranteed meal."

PROMPT · Should schools adopt a four-day school week? Develop your own position.

Thesis. A four-day school week is worth adopting — but only where districts treat the savings as a down payment on students, not a way to quietly do less. The structure can stretch tight budgets and lift morale, yet it risks abandoning the very students who most need a fifth day, so its value depends entirely on what a district does with the time and money it frees up.

Economic case. The financial argument is real. Four-day districts cut busing and utility costs by up to a fifth (Source A), and at least one rural district funneled those savings straight into higher teacher pay (Source E). In places where a school is one bad budget cycle from cutting programs entirely, that flexibility isn't a luxury — it's survival.

The cost few mention. But savings on a balance sheet can hide costs borne by families. Working parents are left scrambling for childcare on the day off (Source B), and low-income students lose a guaranteed daily meal (Source F). A policy that balances the district's books by shifting the burden onto its poorest families hasn't solved a problem; it has relocated it.

Qualification. Long-term evidence keeps the question open: over three years, one district saw attendance and teacher retention actually improve (Source G). That suggests the four-day week isn't inherently harmful — its outcomes track with whether a district reinvests in the people inside the building.

So. The four-day week is neither a gimmick nor a cure. It is a lever, and levers move in whatever direction you push them. Adopt it where the savings buy better teaching and protected services for vulnerable students; reject it where it's only a quieter way to spend less on children.

FRQ 3 · Argument

No sources. Just you and what you know.

Argument asks you to build your own position using evidence from history, literature, current events, personal observation, or broader knowledge. The catch: your examples have to be specific and you have to explain them.

Your evidence toolbox — stock it before exam day

⌛

History

Movements, turning points, decisions and their consequences.

❧

Literature

Characters and conflicts that dramatize big ideas.

◎

Current events

Recent, verifiable examples — not half-remembered headlines.

✦

Personal experience

Used precisely, a real observation can carry a paragraph.

⚛

Science & tech

Discoveries, trade-offs, unintended effects.

⚑

Sports

Teams, rivalries, and the logic of competition.

$

Business & economics

Companies, incentives, booms and busts.

⌂

School & community

What you've actually watched play out around you.

✕ Weak evidence

"Many people work hard, so success is possible."

No one specific, no event, no reasoning. It could be glued onto any essay ever written.

✓ Strong evidence

"During the Civil Rights Movement, organized protest showed that individual courage matters most when it's connected to collective action."

A real moment, a precise claim, and reasoning baked in. It can only support one argument — yours.

PROMPT · Is it more important to prioritize individual freedom or collective responsibility?

Individual freedom and collective responsibility are usually framed as enemies, but the healthiest societies treat them as partners: freedom is what makes responsibility a choice rather than an order, and responsibility is what keeps one person's freedom from becoming another's cage. The question isn't which to pick, but how to keep them in tension.

Consider the polio vaccine campaigns of the 1950s. Vaccination was, technically, an individual medical decision — yet it worked only because millions made the same choice at once, protecting infants too young to be vaccinated themselves. Freedom and obligation weren't opposed; the free choice of individuals produced a collective shield no law could have manufactured as quickly.

The counterexample is just as instructive. When "freedom" is treated as freedom from any obligation at all — the driver who refuses a seatbelt law, the factory that refuses to limit what it dumps upstream — one person's liberty quietly spends down everyone else's. The freedom is real; it's simply being charged to someone else's account.

So the more important value is the one a given moment is missing. A society drifting toward conformity needs its dissenters; a society fracturing into pure self-interest needs to be reminded it is a society at all. Prioritizing one permanently over the other is how both are eventually lost.

Defensible thesis

Takes a real position

Refuses the either/or and stakes a claim about tension — something a reader could push back on.

Specific evidence

A precise example

Names a real campaign and a concrete mechanism — not "history shows us…"

Counterargument

Tests the claim

Brings in the opposing case and explains it instead of ignoring it.

Bigger meaning

Earns sophistication

Resolves the tension with a genuine insight — the value you need depends on the moment.

Read this twice

What kills your score

None of these are about talent. They're habits — and every single one is fixable once you can name it.

Score-killers spotted 0
✕

Summarizing instead of analyzing

Retelling what the text says, not explaining how or why it works.

Fix → For every quote, answer "so what does this do?"
✕

Dropping quotes without explaining

A quote sits there, orphaned, and you move on.

Fix → Quote, then spend two sentences on its effect.
✕

Writing a vague thesis

"There are many factors" tells the grader nothing.

Fix → Name your position and your reasoning in one line.
✕

Listing rhetorical devices

"The author uses ethos, pathos, and logos." A checklist isn't analysis.

Fix → Pick fewer choices and go deeper on each.
✕

Using sources one by one

"Source A says… Source B says…" — a book report, not synthesis.

Fix → Build paragraphs around ideas, pulling sources in.
✕

Generic argument examples

"Throughout history, people have…" supports nothing in particular.

Fix → Use one specific moment you can actually explain.
✕

Ignoring the prompt

Writing the essay you wanted instead of the one asked for.

Fix → Underline the prompt's verbs; answer those exactly.
✕

Trying to sound fancy

Burying a thin idea under thesaurus words.

Fix → Clear writing beats fancy writing every time.

Answer the prompt directly — its verbs are your instructions.
